There's a real satisfaction in crossing something off a list.

You said you'd do the thing. You did the thing. There's a small clean hit that arrives with it. It's earned.

It's worth looking at where that satisfaction is rooted, though. Oftentimes, it sits on performance. There's nothing wrong with performing, even at a very high level.

But what happens on the day you don't perform as well as you thought you could? What happens when the thing you said you'd do stays undone, for any of the myriad reasons a life produces?

If the whole structure rests on performance, a day like that costs more than a day.

Nobody playing basketball is thinking about gravity during the game.

Gravity is a rule of that game, arguably the most (invisible) foundational one in play. Change the gravitational ratio and everything about how the game gets played changes with it. Nobody on the court is thinking about that, because it's so woven into the game that it stopped reading as a rule at all.

Attention goes to the strategic layer instead, the malleable part, the place where you choose this play or that one. That layer feels like choice. It is choice.

It's a much smaller layer than it appears from the inside.

We think we have more choice than we do in the game we're already playing. The rules we forgot were in play only start to surface when we begin changing how we play.

A few years back I made a digital product. I had fun making it.

What I can see now is the expectation sitting underneath it. I thought it was going to do very well. At the time I'd have called that ambition. Doing it the "right" way. The thing a serious person does.

The expectation got woven into the creation. The energy something is made in ends up inside the thing itself. You can't see the weave while your own hands are still moving.

More recently I made a whole run of products. I had fun with those too. The difference was that I knew what they were for, I knew I was leaving breadcrumbs and I held no fixed idea of what any of it needed to become.

I don't read the first one as a mistake. It was part of the journey.

I excavated the old rules of the game to the depth I could reach at the time. I'm doing the same thing right now at whatever depth is available to me now. I expect to read this paragraph differently in a couple of years.

We're always in the middle of the story. There's no definitive arrival, so there's no summit to stand on and look down from with a better opinion of yourself.

So how do you tell the difference?

"This step feels flat" is ambiguous on its own. Sometimes flat is real information. Sometimes flat is the unglamorous middle of something worth finishing. A quest carries a lot of unglamorous middle.

The tell isn't the flatness. The tell is the justification.

When you're excited about something, you're not being strategic about the excitement. You either feel it or you don't. Nobody builds a case for the things that genuinely pull them.

Ask a kid at the end of recess why they chose to play today. You'll get a blank look, or something delightfully unserious. They aren't going to tell you that play accelerates their learning or helps them balance their emotions. The question might be a little ridiculous to them. They're right.

So when you catch yourself assembling a reason, a chain of because-this-leads-to-that, that's the thing worth pausing on. Genuine pull doesn't file a defense.

Here's something to chew on. Digest if it tastes good.

Adventure can live in the most seemingly mundane parts of life. You don't have to build some sweeping epic to approach things this way. That misread is what keeps people waiting for a life dramatic enough to deserve it.

Look around your morning. How could you drink your coffee with a little "quest energy"? How could you take one ordinary thing on today's list and approach it as something you're exploring rather than something you're clearing off a to-do list?

It changes the texture of the thing. The texture is most of it.

A day can run as a sequence of [playgrounds to explore](https://www.infinitegameos.io/concepts/playgrounds-of-exploration) instead of a list to get through. Do it with small things and the big things show up as a by-product. The little gripes turn into a difficult puzzle you get to tinker with rather than something charging at you.

That's a different approach entirely from grinding toward an arrival point that was never there.

You get to play with it. You get to move along your own level of mastery. You get to keep playing, which was the only part that was ever the point.

Most of us inherited the rules of [the game we're playing with our work](https://www.lanebelone.com/blog/f/what-game-of-business-do-you-want-to-play) without ever agreeing to them.
